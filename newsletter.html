Attack-SAM: Towards Evaluating Adversarial Robustness of Segment Anything Model

2023-05-01 15:08:17+00:00

['Chenshuang Zhang', 'Chaoning Zhang', 'Taegoo Kang', 'Donghun Kim', 'Sung-Ho Bae', 'In So Kweon']

The Segment Anything Model (SAM) is becoming increasingly popular in the field of computer vision due to its remarkable success across various tasks. However, there is a critical concern- its vulnerability to adversarial attacks. Such attacks can manipulate a model to predict wrongly without being noticed by human beings. This vulnerability can be problematic while using deep models in security-sensitive applications. A research team is exploring this concern and has attempted to identify the adversarial robustness of SAM in an in-depth study. According to the findings, SAM can be easily fooled by white-box attacks while demonstrating resilience to some extent under the black-box setting. This study is the first of its kind to indicate comprehensively the type of adversarial attacks that can subvert the SAM model. While the research is ongoing, more results and findings are anticipated. You can stay up-to-date on the latest developments by visiting the following website: https://github.com/chenshuang-zhang/attack-sam. The results from this study will be useful in further research regarding adversarial attacks and their impact on foundation models.

Patent Mining by Extracting Functional Analysis Information Modelled As Graph Structure: A Patent Knowledge-base Collaborative Building Approach

2023-04-29 17:34:11+00:00

['Manal E. Helal']

Patent mining is an effective technique for extracting valuable information from patents related to design innovations. In a recent research paper, a team of researchers proposed an automated graph data modelling method for extracting functional representations from patents to build a semantic database of mechanical designs. The proposed method has several benefits over traditional techniques. Firstly, the schema-free characteristic of the proposed graph modelling enables the ontology to evolve and generalise to upper ontologies across technology domains and to specify lower ontologies to more specific domains. Graph modelling benefits from enhanced performance of deep queries across many levels of relationships and interactions and provides efficient storage. Secondly, graph modelling also enables visualisation libraries to use the graph data structure immediately, avoiding the need for graph extraction programs from relational databases. Moreover, Patent/Design comparisons are computed using search queries using counting of overlaps of different levels and weights. The proposed work has produced the PatMine SolidWorks Add-in for comparing annotated CAD designs with patents and highlights overlapping design concepts. The patent annotation extracts its functional analysis, representing its structure as geometric feature interactions. Additional features such as full-text search and semantic search of the PatMine patents database are available, and graph analytic methods and machine learning algorithms are enabled and can be implemented as plug-ins in future work. The proposed approach integrates multiple technologies, such as graph data modelling, natural language processing, and machine learning. This technique has significant potential to revolutionize patent mining, enabling more effective and efficient patent analysis in a variety of fields through the creation of collaborative patent knowledge-base.

['Jingfeng Yang', 'Hongye Jin', 'Ruixiang Tang', 'Xiaotian Han', 'Qizhang Feng', 'Haoming Jiang', 'Bing Yin', 'Xia Hu']

Large Language Models (LLMs) have been changing the landscape of Natural Language Processing (NLP) and revolutionizing downstream tasks. However, using LLMs effectively requires expertise and knowledge about their models, data, and applications. This paper presents a practical guide to using LLMs for practitioners and end-users operating with these models in their NLP downstream tasks. The paper discusses the GPT- and BERT-style LLMs, covers the influence of pre-training data, training data, and test data, and provides detailed insights into the use cases and non-use cases of LLMs in various NLP tasks such as knowledge-intensive tasks, natural language understanding tasks, natural language generation tasks, emergent abilities, and considerations for specific tasks. The authors provide relevant use cases and applications of LLMs, as well as non-use cases that illustrate how their limitations represent significant challenges in real-world scenarios. They also emphasize the critical role of data and outline fundamental considerations affecting the implementation of LLMs. For instance, they delve into the impact of spurious biases and other significant factors that influence deploying LLMs successfully. By presenting the guide, the authors aim to equip researchers and practitioners with valuable insights and best practices for working with LLMs. They also offer a curated list of practical guide resources to enhance the understanding and deploy these models seamlessly. In conclusion, this paper establishes a foundational guide for working with LLMs in real-world scenarios, enabling successful implementation across various NLP tasks.
